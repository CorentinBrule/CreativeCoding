    NOVEMBER 4, 2016

    10 Things to Do Now in NYC

    Compiled by

    Louis Lucero II

    It’s a big city, with plenty to do, see, hear and watch. This guide is a sampling of cultural highlights taking place in New York this weekend and over the week ahead. And there’s much more where these came from.

    Pop & Rock

    Get to Know the Latest Iteration of a Rock Band

    Foals at United Palace Theater

    The British rock group Foals started out in 2005 as a scrappy outfit blending tricky rhythms and jittery guitar melodies with the piercing vocal yelps of its frontman, Yannis Philippakis. But since the group signed with Warner Bros. and began playing bigger performance spaces, its sound has become more complex: The album “What Went Down,” released in 2015, delved into the expansive rock stylings of U2 and Coldplay. For its performance at 8 p.m. on Friday, the band will be joined by Bear Hands and Kiev. KEVIN O’DONNELL

    Museums & Galleries

    Walk Among Artworks in Open Air

    ‘Dennis Oppenheim: Terrestrial Studio’ at Storm King Art Center

    From Minimalist earthworks created in remote places in the 1960s to Pop-Surrealist public sculptures for urban settings in the 1990s and 2000s, Dennis Oppenheim (1938-2011) was dedicated to the proposition that art should be open to the world rather than cloistered in galleries and museums. So it is appropriate that he should be this year’s featured artist at Storm King, in New Windsor, N.Y., where sculptures by Alexander Calder, Sol LeWitt, Louise Nevelson, Alice Aycock and scores of other famous artists dot the beautiful 500-acre landscape. Through Nov. 13, the exhibition presents seven large-scale outdoor works and a selection of drawings, collages and smaller sculptures indoors. KEN JOHNSON

    Movies & Film Series

    Build Up Your Fluency With a Terse Filmic Language

    Bresson on Cinema at BAM Rose Cinemas

    Robert Bresson (1901-99) is one of only a few directors who could legitimately claim to have invented their own cinematic language. His clipped editing rarely shows more than is needed; his use of sound is so precise that it attunes the viewer to every footstep. His Spartan approach perfectly suits his studies of physical or spiritual isolation, including “Pickpocket” (Friday through Sunday), “A Man Escaped” (Nov. 12) and “Diary of a Country Priest” (Nov. 13). But if Bresson’s austere style sets him apart, this Brooklyn Academy of Music series aims to show that he was also engaged in a dialogue with other filmmakers; it puts Bresson’s own films alongside some of his personal favorites by other directors, and additional movies that might be seen as having influenced his theories. BEN KENIGSBERG

    JAZZ

    Listen to a Jazz Band Under New, but Trusted, Leadership

    Charlie Haden’s Liberation Music Orchestra at Jazz Standard

    This revolutionary big band has soldiered on, to one degree or another, since the death of its founder, the great bassist Charlie Haden, in 2014. Now led solely by his longtime collaborator Carla Bley, it has a fine new album, “Time/Life,” named after a tender dirge she wrote in his memory. And it’s no coincidence that the band, stocked with aces like the saxophonists Loren Stillman and Tony Malaby, sets up shop in the run-up to Election Day, with sets through Sunday. NATE CHINEN

    Theater

    Watch as Others Watch the Clock

    ‘Miles for Mary’ at the Bushwick Starr

    The most hellish eternal torture Dante never got around to mentioning — the staff meeting that never ends — is brought to vividly exasperating life by the theater troupe the Mad Ones. Directed by Lila Neugebauer, with a tightly interdependent ensemble, this portrait of a quorum of high school teachers, planning a charity telethon, wittily plumbs the dysfunction in group dynamics (1:45). Unlike some meetings, this production will eventually draw to a close, on Nov. 12. BEN BRANTLEY

    Comedy

    Enjoy Humor Wrung From an Unflinching Look at Life

    Kyle Kinane at Carolines

    His gruff energy and laid-back demeanor mask Mr. Kinane’s skills as a storyteller and joke writer, one who is at his finest when dissecting the worst experiences of his own life. His run at Carolines begins Thursday and will continue through the weekend. ELISE CZAJKOWSKI

    Classical Music

    Acquaint Yourself With a Celebrity of the Classical World

    New Jersey Symphony Orchestra at the New Jersey Performing Arts Center

    Following her debut last week as music director of the New Jersey Symphony Orchestra, the internationally respected conductor Xian Zhang leads a program of works on Saturday and Sunday that includes Beethoven’s Triple Concerto, Haydn’s Symphony No. 102 and Strauss’s “Der Rosenkavalier” Suite. ANTHONY TOMMASINI

    Around Town

    Celebrate a Culture That Relishes Life

    Latin American Cultural Week

    This celebration of Latin American art returns for an 11th year of performances, art shows and talks at locations around New York. Highlights include the opening-night gala, which goes until 2 a.m. and features a dance class, a concert and a midnight show by the tango superstar Gabriel Missé and Carla Espinoza. (And don’t be deterred by the word “gala”: Tickets are $30 in advance, and $40 in cash at the door. Saturday at 7 p.m., Clemente Soto Vélez Cultural Center, 107 Suffolk Street, between Rivington and Delancey Streets, Lower East Side.) Additional events — and there are a lot — are online. JOSHUA BARONE

    See other cultural festivals, discussions, tours and more.

    For Children

    Have a Closer Look at Films Meant to Open Children’s Eyes

    My First Film Fest at Walter Reade Theater

    You wouldn’t expect someone who had just created a movie festival for children — especially one that began on Thursday with a sneak preview of DreamWorks Animation’s cheerful-looking “Trolls” — to heave a sigh and exclaim, “Oh, my God, so many dark films!” Yet that was how Florence Almozini, associate director of programming for the Film Society of Lincoln Center, recently characterized her latest venture.

    But dark doesn’t mean despairing. For My First Film Fest, the society’s inaugural festival for young people and its new initiative to attract the next generation of viewers, Ms. Almozini sought works that pose challenges, confront problems and capture real-world messiness. She also wanted the event, which runs through Tuesday, to appeal to teenagers, too.

    “What stays with you sometimes are the films with deeper issues,” she said.

    Dance

    Take Part in a Challenge to Solo Dance

    ‘Fear in Porcelain’ at the Chocolate Factory

    Sam Kim has said she finds solos “gross” because they demand that audiences focus their attention on a single person. In other words, they’re inherently narcissistic. But, she asks, “What if the solo becomes the antithesis of ego, in fact, destroys it?” In her work “Fear in Porcelain,” which she has been developing for two years, Ms. Kim and three other performers will try to do just that by heightening the audience’s voyeuristic role as they wriggle and writhe (1:00). Performances will begin on Wednesday and continue through Nov. 19. BRIAN SCHAEFER
