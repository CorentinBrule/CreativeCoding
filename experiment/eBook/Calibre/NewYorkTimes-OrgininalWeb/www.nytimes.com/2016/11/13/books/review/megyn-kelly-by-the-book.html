    Megyn Kelly: By the Book

    NOV. 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    The Fox News anchor and author of “Settle for More” says “Bridget Jones’s Diary” reads “like my own journals from pretty much every phase of my life and made me laugh so hard I cried.”

    What books are currently on your night stand?

    “Maestra,” by L. S. Hilton; “The Wright Brothers,” by David McCullough; and “The First Tycoon: The Epic Life of Cornelius Vanderbilt,” by T. J. Stiles. I’m loving “The Wright Brothers” in particular — the story of how these scrappy brothers with no connection to great wealth, fancy education or people in power went down to Kitty Hawk and through sheer will, determination and natural ingenuity changed the world.

    What’s the last great book you read?

    “City of Thieves,” by David Benioff. I am a fan of Benioff’s, not only because I love “Game of Thrones” (which I watch with one hand over my eyes since I can’t take violence on the screen) but because his writing is tight and packs a punch and yet seems to include a dose of whimsy. I like writers who don’t waste my time or make me work too hard.

    Your husband, Douglas Brunt, is a novelist. Do you read and help edit his novels for him? Do you two share the same tastes in books?

    I get the first read of all of Doug’s books, and it is a privilege I treat seriously. He’s a beautiful writer — if you read his debut novel (a New York Times best seller — forgive my pride), “Ghosts of Manhattan,” you’ll know why I love Doug. Doug is a voracious reader — mostly literary fiction and historical works. I’ll dabble in those kinds of books too, but I do so much reading for work every day, I find it hard to delve into anything too serious for fun. I’ve also usually got some self-help book in my bag — at the moment it’s Arianna Huffington’s “The Sleep Revolution.” I’m going to dive right into it as soon as I feel better rested.

    How do you like to read? Paper or electronic? One book at a time or simultaneously? Morning or night?

    I like actual books. When I use a tablet for book reading, I miss having the ability to thumb through the pages to easily compare and contrast. I also like the feel and the smell of real books — it’s like walking around with a treasure. Reading off a tablet is less of an escape for me — it takes me too close (physically and mentally) to all of the other things I should probably be doing. I never read in the mornings — what mother of a 3-, 5- and 7-year-old reads in the morning? I read at night or while traveling. Sometimes I will come home from work around midnight and read the books I know my husband read to our children before their bedtime so I can be up to speed if they want to talk about it. (O.K., O.K., it’s possible I also find the stories of those poor Baudelaire children compelling.) If a book bores me, I have no qualms about retiring it early and moving on. Life is too short to lumber through bad books or bad cocktails.

    What was the best thing you read about this year’s election cycle?

    Maureen Dowd’s “The Year of Voting Dangerously.” Maureen is always brave in her columns, and reading so many in a collection only underscores how honest and merciless she is — qualities I admire in any columnist, or journalist. My own book, “Settle for More” (which hits stores on Nov. 15) offers some fascinating back stories to the 2016 election, including my time on the receiving end of Donald Trump’s attentions, which I think may really surprise some folks.

    What’s the best book ever written about the law? The best book you read in law school?

    When it comes to writing about the law, I’m a big fan of author (and Harvard Law School graduate) Scott Turow. “Presumed Innocent” was one of my favorite legal thrillers even before the movie (starring Harrison Ford), and the best book I have ever read about law school is Turow’s “One L,” which perfectly captures the absurdity of law students’ obsessive self-flagellation and their insatiable, impossible drive for affirmation from those who instruct them.

    Tell us about the last book that made you laugh.

    The last book that made me laugh out loud was Amy Poehler’s “Yes Please,” which also includes a fair amount of helpful life advice, so — bonus! — I felt productive reading it. But the book that had me in stitches more than any other, ever, in my life was easily “Bridget Jones’s Diary,” which read like my own journals from pretty much every phase of my life and made me laugh so hard I cried.

    The last book that made you cry for other reasons?

    “When Breath Becomes Air,” by Paul Kalanithi. Its exploration of mortality begins as clinical and philosophical and ends in such a simple, lovely place of acceptance despite the inability to understand. Also, pretty much anything by Anna Quindlen.

    What book might people be surprised to find on your shelves?

    The 40-year retrospective on Penthouse centerfolds. The then-C.E.O. of Penthouse sat next to me on an airplane about seven years ago and asked me if I would talk to his wife on his cellphone (she was apparently a fan). I was happy to do it, and as a thank you, he later sent me the book, which is quite large and beautifully bound, and came in a box that had “PENTHOUSE” written all over every square inch of it. I’ve never seen the mailman so intent to linger.

    What’s the best book you’ve ever received as a gift?

    “Einstein’s Dreams,” by Alan Lightman. As someone who never took physics (never mind at Lightman’s M.I.T.), I loved the hope this collection about time provided about what might have been, what might be now and what may yet come. I found it inspiring and, even, comforting.

    What kind of reader were you as a child? Which childhood books and authors stick with you most?

    I was a casual reader and an avid writer. I kept a journal and found it very cathartic. I loved Nancy Drew books, anything involving a mystery or a monster. Later I loved the Judy Blume books, which were quite an education. My favorite book was Roald Dahl’s “Charlie and the Chocolate Factory,” which went on to become my favorite movie (renamed “Willy Wonka and the Chocolate Factory”). Its hopeful message of bad actors not getting ahead and the good kid who thinks of others living happily ever after inspired me to do and be better.

    What do you plan to read next?

    “Trophy Son,” my husband’s next novel, which comes out this spring. It’s about the insane pressure we put on our kids to excel, and a young man’s struggle to know — and be — himself in this crazy, overscheduled, pressure-filled world we have somehow come to accept as “normal.”
