    Review: Shakespeare’s Take on the Game of Thrones

    Next Wave Festival: Kings of War

    • NYT Critics’ Pick
    • Off Broadway, Drama, Play, Shakespeare
    • 4 hrs. and 23 min.
    • Closing Date: November 6, 2016
    • Brooklyn Academy of Music - Howard Gilman Opera House, 30 Lafayette St.
    • 718-636-4100

    By BEN BRANTLEYNOV. 4, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    During the more than four thrilling hours of Ivo van Hove’s “Kings of War,” an omnibus adaptation of five Shakespearean history plays at the Brooklyn Academy of Music, you may feel that those disturbing noises coming from the stage are just echoes of what you’re hearing in your own head these days. Sometimes there’s the amplified ticking of a clock, a bursting heartbeat, a mismatched thrum of tentacular chords or a brass fanfare sliding into sinister dissonance.

    In every case, though, the sound (dazzlingly realized in Eric Sleichim’s music) is that of a nation slowly cracking apart amid corruption, factionalism and political viciousness, as the oversize egos of would-be rulers collide with toxic trickle-down consequences.

    That would seem to be a list of exactly what you’ve had enough of during this grimy election season. But sometimes having your darkest anxieties externalized into gripping, scary theater can be a splendid means of catharsis. So I strongly suggest that before you hit the polls on Tuesday, you book a therapeutic seat at the Howard Gilman Opera House before “Kings of War,” which opened on Thursday night, closes on Sunday.

    In this cold-eyed, hot-blooded work from the Toneelgroep Amsterdam company, political cynicism moves with the heady rush of sexual passion. Settle in for a long session of shivers, gasps and, despite yourself, satisfied smiles. “Kings of War” takes the home-viewing pleasures associated with serial television portraits of cutthroat schemers, like those in “House of Cards” and “The Sopranos,” and magnifies them to the proportions of grand opera.

    Mr. van Hove, perhaps the most influential director of his generation in international theater, is on an astonishing upward trajectory just now, going from strength to strength. In the last Broadway season, he delivered two powerful interpretations of Arthur Miller classics, “A View From the Bridge” and “The Crucible,” in productions that grabbed the viscera.

    Like those works, “Kings of War” pulses with the heightened dread you associate with Greek tragedy. Unlike those revivals, Mr. van Hove’s latest offering seldom invites us to identify with the doomed characters portrayed here.

    This is big-picture theater, which takes a long view down the corridors of power to anatomize the psyches of the men and women who determine the fate of nations. For the most part, you’re likely to feel more terror than pity (except, possibly, for the unseen millions who are ruled by these narcissists).

    Only in the production’s second half are we treated to the sort of deep-probing characterization that we expect from Shakespeare. And, yes, you may even experience a nauseated taste of compassion for the man who dominates the play’s final tense 90 minutes of calculation and carnage.

    His name? Richard III, also known as that “poisonous, hunchbacked toad” and “hound of hell.” As embodied with limp, crook and humble mien by Hans Kesting in a dazzlingly original performance, he is a psychopath to remember forever. He is also, in the context of the polluted scheme of things, the inevitable culmination of every leader who has gone before him.

    “Kings of War” condenses and conflates five plays by Shakespeare (and, to give credit where it’s due, Christopher Marlowe, too), rearranged chronologically not according to when they were written, but when the events they portray took place. So, in the adaptation by Bart van den Eynde and Peter van Kraaij, we begin with “Henry V” (with a prologue cadged from “Henry IV, Part II”); segue into the three “Henry VI” plays (which recent scholarship has concluded that Marlowe helped write); and end the evening’s longest segment with “Richard III.”

    Though they trace a decades-spanning genealogy that links pretty much everyone onstage to everybody else, these plays are radically different in their tone and language. What Mr. van Hove and his adapters have done is to strip the texts down to their political marrow, concentrating on the dynamics of waging war and other, more devious modes of power seeking.

    The usual throngs of soldiers, rebels and choral citizens seldom materialize. (There’s no Joan of Arc, no Jack Cade.) We are largely confined to sealed environments, impeccably designed and lighted by Jan Versweyveld, in which the gravest threats come not from without but within the bosom of friends and family.

    This is not a production for savoring Shakespeare’s language. For one thing, the cast is speaking almost entirely in Dutch and the (very audience-friendly) projected supertitles (from a translation by Rob Klinkenberg) tend to iron the music right out of the plays’ more memorable lines. (“I am not in the giving vein” becomes “I am not in a giving mood.”)

    Yet, in this telling, another, starker kind of poetry emerges, carved by a through-line of exigency, ruthlessness and self-advancement. The contemporary set evokes the throne room as war room, replete with the latest in cartographic and surveillance technology; a portentous battery of telephones (yep, one’s red); and, oh, yes, television monitors, including one immense paneled screen, which allows you access to what’s happening behind the scenes.

    (Warning: it often ain’t pretty. That’s where they keep their corpses.)

    Working with the video designer Tal Yarden, Mr. van Hove deploys the medium to newly unsettling ends. Our leading men here, after all, are used to existing in televised close-ups, and we see the successive coronations (five, I believe), staged on a conveniently portable red carpet, simultaneously in two and three dimensions. (Not surprisingly, they look a lot more graceful on screen.)

    But close-ups of the kings also materialize when they regard themselves in mirrors, playing to our tendency, now ingrained, to read television close-ups of people in power for psychological tells. (Think of how you watched the recent presidential debates.)

    Levels of awareness and primal instincts are meticulously embodied in the astute performances of the 14-strong ensemble, most of whose members play several roles. That they’re all infected by power lust is beyond question; it’s just how aware they are of this contamination.

    Henry V (Ramsey Nasr) is still able to delude himself that he’s a patriotic man for (if not of) the people. Yet when he’s thwarted, he develops a Hitlerian sputter. And his crowd-rousing St. Crispin’s Day Speech comes across as brutally militaristic.

    His successor, Henry VI (Eelco Smits), is a mewling, bespectacled child king and a pawn to be manipulated and mauled by, among others, his wife (Janni Goslinga) and her lover (Robert de Hoog). Then there’s the poker-faced, Don Corleone-esque Edward (Bart Slegers), who oversees Henry VI’s unseating with chilling sang-froid.

    And, finally, we come to Edward’s brother, the Duke of Gloucester, who has not only the usual humped torso (though it’s well-toned beneath his shirt) but also a birthmark on his face, like a stain of spilled red wine. In his too-tight, mismatched clothes, he suggests a creepy, socially challenged uncle in a family that has more or less learned to accept him.

    But when he turns to himself in the mirror — to which he pitches his soliloquies — it’s clear that there’s nothing harmless about him. We see (via video projection) what the mirror sees: a hungry man who can commune with, and draw sustenance from, himself alone.

    Heaven help those destined to be attracted to — or, worse, governed by — the man who is his own best (and only) friend. “Kings of War” chillingly suggests that this description applies to pretty much everyone who makes it to the top.
