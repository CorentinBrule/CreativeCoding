    There Are No Wrong Thanksgiving Wines

    Wines of The Times

    By ERIC ASIMOV NOV. 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    No sooner did last Thanksgiving end than here we are again, deciding what to serve, how to cook it and what to drink. It worked out pretty well in 2015, didn’t it?

    People will stuff their faces, just as they always do. Family and friends will abound, and though we may occasionally complain about grudges and petty differences, the gathering will be pretty fine in the end. It may seem a bit daunting if guests are all coming over to your place, but as Caliban said, “Be not afeard.” It worked out great last year, and it will again this year.

    The wine is the easy part. I know this because every year since 2004 the wine panel has held a mock Thanksgiving in advance of the holiday to test out a series of wines with a meal resembling a typical feast.

    Each of us brings a white and a red, and sometimes a sparkler or a rosé, and we see how they go with the food. Over the years, we have boiled down our thoughts about which wines are best. Let me offer this concentrated nugget of wisdom.

    You cannot go wrong.

    Whichever wines you serve, they will be well received. Your guests will be delighted. You will be pleased. Nobody but incorrigible grouches will complain or render their unsolicited critical judgments.

    As with so much that surrounds Thanksgiving, the apprehension over which wines to serve arises because of the ambient anxiety inherent in entertaining, especially if you expect a large crowd. The wine panel — Florence Fabricant, Julia Moskin, Pete Wells and me — is here to help. For the food, here’s a handy planner. For the wine, read on. Our assumptions are simple: We imagine a large crowd, so you won’t want to spend a lot of money on wine. We cap our choices at $25, and you can spend half of that if you like and still drink very well.

    If you are serving Thanksgiving for four, do what you would ordinarily do for a fine meal: haul out your best wines, serve them in your most fragile glassware and pair bottles to each course.

    For our feast, we don’t worry about matching wines to particular dishes. The meal is too varied and personal for that, and large crowds guarantee a motley assortment of foods.

    So our recommendation is this: Pick one red and one white, and have plenty of bottles on hand, more than you think you need. One bottle per drinking guest is a safe estimate. It may sound like too much, but it will ensure that you don’t run out. If it bothers you to have extra bottles lying around, give them out as parting gifts.

    Do not worry if you don’t have enough stemware. Thanksgiving is not the time for these sorts of concerns. Serve wine in tumblers, if you like. It’s actually great fun to drink wine informally that way.

    Still not enough glasses? Good paper cups are not the end of the world. I recommend them over plastic because great fields of marine debris torment me. If you cannot resist the plastic, do not — I repeat, do not — use plastic stems. They will come apart unexpectedly, dumping red wine in places where you emphatically do not want it. Use plastic tumblers instead.

    Which wines to choose? In our experience, the best choices are light-bodied, nimble, versatile wines that can be consumed with many varied foods over a span of hours. That means wines that are not high in alcohol, not oaky and not particularly tannic. They ought to be refreshing and energetic; you want wines with lively acidity.

    In practice, that leaves many options. Beaujolais is always a great choice for a red, and bone-dry sauvignon blancs can be delicious whites. Rieslings are wonderful, as are any number of Italian reds like barberas or Chiantis and Italian whites like fianos, falanghinas and carricantes. Loire reds are fine choices, as are mencías from Spain. Zinfandels are often recommended, although I usually find them too big for the long haul, unless you can find one under 14 percent alcohol.

    The wine panel along with our tasting coordinator, Bernie Kirsch, brought none of these wines. And yet, the choices were almost all delicious. As I said, you can’t go wrong. Of the 10 bottles, one wasn’t even a wine. It was a still cider, about 6 percent alcohol like a lager beer, brought by Pete in place of a red because, he said, he was looking for a fruit flavor other than the grape.

    He chose a German cider, or apfelwein, from Weidmann & Groh, because he feared a sparkling cider would be too gassy, and it was the only still cider he could find. It was light, fresh and floral, unusually flavored possibly because apples were blended with speierling, an applelike fruit that is a common addition in Germany.

    I love the idea of cider at Thanksgiving. This was good but maybe a bit sweet to drink over many hours. I would probably opt for a dry cider.

    “In the depths of my self-delusion, I thought I could trick you into thinking it was wine,” he told us. Not these seasoned pros.

    Pete also brought a white made by Dr. Konstantin Frank in the Finger Lakes, a 2014 rkatsiteli, a rare grape that originated in the country of Georgia. It was floral and exuberant and, as Pete put it, “hyperactive with the food.” Not bad at all, just possibly fatiguing.

    Of the nine wines, seven were American (four from New York). I don’t put much stock in the insistence of some that only American wines should be consumed on this American holiday; drinking what you like is the most American thing you can do. This year we happened to be thinking American and local.

    Nonetheless, our two favorite wines, both chosen by Bernie, were from Burgundy: the savory, textured 2014 Chablis from Isabelle & Denis Pommier, and the bright, juicy 2014 Bourgogne pinot noir from Digioia-Royer. These are Thanksgiving classics. You don’t have to look to Burgundy for pinot noir and chardonnay, the grape of Chablis. Just seek out restrained, mild versions.

    Julia picked our No. 2 white and red: a nutty, earthy 2013 pinot blanc, made by Red Hook Winery in Brooklyn with grapes from the North Fork of Long Island, and a pale, herbal 2013 pinot noir from Ravines of the Finger Lakes.

    Both wines were highly unusual. The pinot blanc seemed to have a touch of sweetness to it with a purely savory aftertaste, while the pinot noir reminded me more of a trousseau from the Jura. Both blossomed with the meal.

    Florence also chose unusual bottles. One was a balanced, brisk 2015 white made of the red pinot noir grape from Amity Vineyards in the Willamette Valley of Oregon.

    I’ve seen these white pinot noirs from Italy, Alsace and, of course, Champagne. The red grapes are treated as if they were being used to make a white wine, meaning the grapes are crushed and the pigment-bearing skins are whisked away. Her red was a bright, lightly tannic 2013 dolcetto from Palmina in the Santa Ynez Valley in California, which does a very good job with Italian grapes.

    That leaves my wines. In the last few years, there has been a revival of American chenin blancs. This floral, textured 2014 version is from Precedent, the personal label of Nathan Kandler, the excellent winemaker at Thomas Fogarty, who got these grapes from Lodi in the Sierra foothills.

    My red was the 2012 White Horse from Bloomer Creek in the Finger Lakes, a wine I’ve always liked. White Horse is a play on Cheval Blanc, the great wine from St.-Émilion, and like Cheval, it’s made of cabernet franc and merlot. It is inexpensive and delicious.

    We admittedly had our share of oddballs at this meal. Chances are you will not find any of these wines precisely. The point is not these particular bottles, but their characteristics: energy, acidity and relatively low alcohol.

    That’s the ideal. Here’s the larger point: Even big bruisers will ultimately be fine. Nobody comes to Thanksgiving for the wines. They come for the other people. As I said, you can’t go wrong.

    ★★★ ISABELLE & DENIS POMMIER CHABLIS 2014 $23

    Savory, subtle, detailed and textured; versatile and delicious. (Polaner Selections, Mount Kisco, N.Y.)

    ★★★ THE RED HOOK WINERY NORTH FORK OF LONG ISLAND PALMER VINEYARD PINOT BLANC 2013 $20

    ★★½ AMITY VINEYARDS WILLAMETTE VALLEY WHITE PINOT NOIR 2015 $20

    ★★½ PRECEDENT LODI BORDEN RANCH CHENIN BLANC SEC 2014 $25

    Spicy and richly textured, with floral, herbal, honey and apple flavors.

    ★★ DR. KONSTANTIN FRANK FINGER LAKES RKATSITELI 2014 $20

    Floral aromas, with exuberant flavors of grapefruit and tropical fruits.

    REDS

    ★★★ DIGIOIA-ROYER BOURGOGNE PINOT NOIR 2014 $25

    Bright, juicy, lightly tannic and delicious with food. (Selection Pas Mal/Becky Wasserman/U.S.A. Imports, New York)

    ★★★ RAVINES FINGER LAKES PINOT NOIR 2013 $25

    Surprisingly pale-colored, subtle and reticent on its own, but fresh, herbal flavors come alive with food.

    ★★★ BLOOMER CREEK FINGER LAKES WHITE HORSE DRY RED 2012 $25

    Spicy, floral and pure, with fresh, earthy flavors of red fruits.

    ★★½ PALMINA SANTA YNEZ VALLEY DOLCETTO 2013 $18

    Rich and lightly tannic, with bright flavors of red and dark fruits.

    CIDER

    ★★½ WEIDMANN & GROH APFELWEIN MIT SPEIERLING FRANKEN, GERMANY $17, 1 liter

    Light and fresh, with a floral aroma, but perhaps a bit too sweet. (Rowan Imports, Queens, N.Y.)

    Recipe pairing: Apple Salad With Walnuts and Brussels Sprouts

    To pour red or white matters little for most dishes on the Thanksgiving menu; either choice can complement. A salad may pose a challenge. White wine usually edges out red. But this apple salad from the Publican, a Chicago restaurant renowned for meat, holds its own against a house red. In assembling the dish, the sous-chef, Ben Truesdell, tore a page from the Waldorf salad, with apples and walnuts — but then he went his own way. It is a sturdy creation that can be made in advance and sit on a buffet without wilting. The cured ham, which is optional, drives the pairing more toward red wine than white. FLORENCE FABRICANT
