    The ’90s Designer Making a Big Comeback — All Over the Runways

    By ALEXANDER FURYNOV. 3, 2016

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story

    Even during his heyday of the early- to mid-’90s, the French designer Jean Colonna was an insider name — but today, his style is all over the runways. In 1993, The Times dubbed him “the popularist of Drugstore Cowboy dressing”: a pithy acknowledgment of his aesthetic, which relied heavily on synthetic fabrics, especially pleather, machine-made lace and machine-sewn sequins, animal prints and brash color combinations of black with violet, hot pink or lipstick red. It looked trashy — it did then, and it does now.

    But that trash looks right, too. Colonna’s ’90s collections feel like ancestors of the kind of clothes being made to great acclaim today by Demna Gvasalia and his teams at Vetements and Balenciaga, with their predilection for “cheap” materials like velour, Lycra or polyester, their sharp, skinny silhouettes and their knowing challenging of fashion’s traditional status symbols. There were also echoes of Colonna’s style at Louis Vuitton and Saint Laurent’s spring shows. It wasn’t copying, or even pastiche, but there is a distinct, discernible connection — a shared sense of color and shape, a frisson of bad taste.

    It is by no means inevitable that, as designers rush to revive a decade, the stars of that selfsame era will be lifted back to prominence. Fashion’s a tough game. Yet you can’t help but feel that Colonna has benefited from the latest flush of nostalgia around the ’90s — he certainly hopes so. He opened a shop in the Marais in 2014, designing clothes under his own name — 12 years since he last showed on the Paris runway, and a decade after his last seasonal offering. But little changed in the interim: Jean Colonna now, and back then, sells a distinctly European breed of grunge he helped pioneer.

    That European — or, more accurately, French — addition to grunge was sex. There’s plenty in what Colonna did, and what he still does. That’s what distinguishes his work from that of his friends and contemporaries Helmut Lang and Martin Margiela, whose approaches to fabric and to the perceived “status” of fashion at the turn of the ’80s were similar to his own — but whose work enjoys wider acclaim and renown than Colonna’s. Perhaps it’s because Margiela worked long enough to see his work lionized, as his early supporters gained influential positions in the fashion hierarchy; perhaps it’s because Lang retired on the crest of his wave and at the height of his influence. But in the ’90s, Colonna was lumped together with them in a group of designers tagged as “Deconstructionists,” whose work in that period radically shifted fashion’s poles. Colonna, like Margiela at the same time (Colonna began his label in 1987, Margiela in 1988), turned jackets inside-out to expose seams and used those unexpected, aforementioned “nonfashion” synthetics to subvert notions of luxury. “It was not ‘we want to change,’ non,” says the now-62-year-old Colonna, referring to that loosely-associated group of so-called deconstructionist designers. “We just wanted to do what we thought we had to do. It was not rebellion, cultural rebellion, not at all.”

    It may not have been intentional, but of course it was cultural rebellion: ripping up the rule book of a decade obsessed with conspicuous consumption and opulence. That is precisely why designers are referencing that moment again, and why it seems to be resonating so loudly. Colonna himself had been trained in haute couture — he studied at the ultratraditional Chambre Syndicale, and worked at the then-staid, haute-couture focused house of Balmain for two years in the ’70s. After the couture-flushed ’80s — with the revival of Chanel, the crowning of Yves Saint Laurent and the triumphant rise (and rapid fall) of Christian Lacroix — Colonna, Lang, Margiela and their ilk offered something else. “We had different vision for the fabrics,” he says today. “We had a different vision for the shape. We had another vision for the woman.” That vision was sometimes difficult for fashion to stomach. He recalls the fact that, when he printed a Nan Goldin image on a fashion invite in the early ’90s, guests actively stayed away. “At that point, it was completely shocking for them.”

    What shocks today? After a few years characterized by hyped-up luxury — with a nominal couture boom to boot — the different aesthetic Colonna offered then, and now, feels a little shocking. But also fresh, exciting, even seductive. The designer himself is trading on the interest in his back catalog — his latest collection, for spring/summer 2017, was styled and modeled by Camille Bidault-Waddington, who also experienced renown in Colonna’s period. The clothes, narrow and simple in silhouette, combine tawdry fabrics like python-printed leather, stockinette knit, tarnished sequins and various layers of shredded schmattes. They’re not pieces exhumed from the archive, but they’re definitely a continuation. “What have I done in the ’90s, which works today?” Colonna asks, hypothetically, then gestures to a nearby rack of clothes. “Voilà. That interests me. Of course there’s some old fashions here,” he allows, flicking at the hangers. “But I have to say, only a few.”

    Vintage Colonna, incidentally, has a roaring secondhand market. “It’s quite expensive today — especially in Japan,” he allows. His clothes, old and new, tap right into what so many other designers are doing, at all levels. Colonna shows me a pair of fur coats from his archives: one is from a ’90s collection; the other is, stitch by stitch, its facsimile, yet bears the label of a well-known mass-market brand and was sold last winter. I can’t say which, as part of Colonna’s recently settled lawsuit. I wonder if Colonna is vexed by other designers making a quick buck on his aesthetic? “I don’t care,” he shrugs, rolling his eyes. “Some try to sell maybe what I say at one point was interesting, but guys, if you don’t have any more ideas, take it. When I saw the first show of Kanye West…” He pauses, frowns. “I said, ‘O.K., if you like, do it.’ But it’s like when you are repeating a recipe and you have fake spices… I know the taste, not the same. It’s not that.”
